# India DPDP (Rules 2025) — how mnemo maps

> **Not legal advice.** This maps mnemo's *technical* capabilities to the
> Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 and the DPDP Rules, 2025. Whether a
> deployment complies is a legal determination. mnemo supplies substrate — an
> append-only, tamper-evident, externally-verifiable memory-write log with
> at-rest encryption and access control — not a compliance certificate. Confirm
> every reference against the current notified text.

## The instrument and its timeline

The **Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023** (DPDPA) is India's data-
protection statute; the operative detail lives in the **DPDP Rules, 2025**. The
Rules phase in: some provisions apply on notification, and the substantive
Data-Fiduciary obligations (security safeguards, breach handling, retention/
erasure, consent-record keeping) become **fully applicable on a later date — the
working figure is 2027-05-13**, roughly eighteen months after the relevant Rules
were notified.

### Timeline hedge (read this)

The 2027-05-13 date reflects the phased-commencement structure of the Rules as
understood at time of writing. The exact date for each obligation depends on the
final notified schedule and any transitional provisions, which can change.
Treat it as provisional and verify against the notified Rules before relying on
it.

## What mnemo provides (verified against the codebase)

| DPDP theme | Obligation (paraphrased) | mnemo mechanism (shipped) | Where |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accountability / records | A Data Fiduciary must be able to demonstrate what it did with personal data | Append-only `agent_events` log; every write/delete is a hash-chained event; `verify_event_integrity` proves the record was not altered after the fact | `query/event_builder.rs`, `hash.rs` |
| Security safeguards (§8(5)) | Reasonable security safeguards to prevent breach | AES-256-GCM at-rest content encryption; RBAC on recall/share; SHA-256 tamper-evidence over the log; permission-safe ANN filtering | `encryption.rs`, `auth.rs`, `mnemo-postgres` `prevent_event_modification` trigger |
| Erasure (§8(7)) | Erase personal data when consent is withdrawn or purpose is served, unless retention is legally required | `ForgetStrategy::HardDelete` (row removed) and `ForgetStrategy::Redact` (content scrubbed to `[REDACTED]`, hash slot retained so the *fact and time* of the write stay auditable) | `query/forget.rs` |
| Retention limitation | Do not keep personal data beyond need | `SoftDelete` + point-in-time `replay { as_of }` let an operator reconstruct and expire on a schedule; deletion is itself logged | `query/forget.rs`, `query/replay.rs` |
| Breach forensics | Reconstruct what happened | Tamper-evident log + causality tracing localise the first altered record and its downstream effects | `query/causality.rs`, `hash.rs::verify_event_chain` |

### The erasure-vs-retention tension is explicit, not hidden

DPDP §8(7) can require **erasure** while EU AI Act Art.26(6) can require
**retention** of the same underlying event for six months. mnemo does not
pretend this away: it ships **both** primitives and **logs which one was used**.
`Redact` is the middle path — it removes the personal content while keeping the
hash-chain slot, so the audit trail ("a record existed here, written at T, later
redacted") survives without retaining the personal data itself. Which primitive
to invoke, and when, is the operator's documented policy decision.

## What mnemo does NOT do

- **Consent management, notice, and grievance redressal** are Data-Fiduciary
  obligations that live at the application/organisation layer, not inside a
  memory store. mnemo records *that* a write happened and under what provenance;
  it does not collect consent or serve notices.
- **It does not decide lawfulness.** Choosing `HardDelete` vs `Redact` vs
  retention, and mapping purposes to retention windows, are legal/policy calls.
- **It is not a compliance certificate.** It addresses the record-keeping,
  security, and erasure *substrate* only.
